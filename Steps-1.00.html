Tips from the General:


  • A KPI by iteself is pointless.  For example, let's say Total Revenue = $4 Million.  Is that good or bad?  You don't know unless you compare it to something.  You can compare it to a Target or you can compare it to a different point in time.  For Exmple March 2018 vs. March 2019.  When reporting on KPI's you always want to give some sense of doing good or bad.  Always use some sort of comparison for your KPI's to drive decision making.  


  • Total Revenue or anything that is just sumed up to get the KPI is usually not a good KPI.   Let's take a scenario for clarification purposes:

                        Store A  - Made $500 this year but just opened 2 months ago

                        Store B -  Made $2k this year and has been opened for 10 months

            Total Revenue (KPI) shows :

                        Store A = $500

                        Store B = $2k

            Revenue per month (KPI) shows:

                        Store A = $250

                        Store B = $200


When you start slicing your KPI's you want to use KPI's that make the numbers as close together as possible.  For example, above for Total Rev, Store A and Store B are $1500 apart when Rev per Month only makes them $50 apart.  

An Even better option would be to use a % if you can because most times your values will be between 0 and 100 which puts the comparisons on better even playing ground.  When you have 2 values in a chart double and triple it's lower counterparts, it takes the value out of the lower couterparts.
